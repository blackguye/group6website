Typhoon Yolanda, also known as Typhoon Haiyan, struck the Philippines in November 2013 as one of the most powerful tropical cyclones on record,
reaching Category 5 super typhoon status with sustained winds of up to 315 km/h (195 mph) and gusts of up to 380 km/h (235 mph). Devastating the 
central regions,including Leyte, Samar, and Eastern Visayas, it caused widespread destruction, resulting in thousands of fatalities, displacing 
millions, and prompting amassive international humanitarian response.
  • Human Losses and Impact: Typhoon Yolanda caused thousands of deaths, particularly in Leyte Province where an estimated 10,000 lives were lost due to
    intense winds and storm surges.
  • Geographical Impact: Eastern Samar and Leyte Provinces experienced heavy rains, floods, landslides, intense winds, and storm surges,
    resulting in extensive damage.
  • Economic Consequences: The region's economy, primarily reliant on coconut farming before the typhoon, suffered further deterioration post-disaster.
  • Socioeconomic Challenges: Eastern Visayas became the poorest region in the Philippines by 2012, with the typhoon exacerbating existing poverty and economic disparities.
  • Infrastructure and Recovery: Extensive infrastructure damage required significant reconstruction efforts, focusing on restoring essential services and livelihoods.
